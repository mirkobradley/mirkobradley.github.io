Bitcoin: The Freedom of Dictators

In 2025 crypto addresses linked to illicit activity received at least 154 billion dollars. A 162 percent increase from the previous year. The main driver was a 694 percent surge in funds received by sanctioned entities, totaling 104 billion dollars.

The protagonists are Russia, Iran, and North Korea.

To understand why these numbers matter, consider what sanctions actually are. Sanctions are punitive measures used by governments and international organizations to punish countries, regimes, or individuals that violate international law or threaten global security. In practice they mean this: your bank accounts are frozen, you cannot transact in dollars or euros, your exports are blocked. It is how the West exerts pressure without firing weapons. Russia after invading Ukraine. Iran for its nuclear program and support of terrorism. North Korea for its nuclear weapons. All under heavy sanctions. All, in 2025, major users of Bitcoin and crypto.

Russia launched A7A5, a ruble-pegged stablecoin. It processed over 93 billion dollars in transactions in 2025, becoming the parallel financial infrastructure that allows Russia to bypass the Western banking blockade. It buys oil, pays suppliers, funds military operations. All beyond the reach of sanctions.

Iran has done the same through proxy networks linked to the IRGC, the Islamic Revolutionary Guard Corps, designated a terrorist organization by the US, EU, and others. These networks moved over 3 billion dollars in crypto in 2025 to finance regional militias, illegal oil sales, and weapons purchases. North Korea took a different approach: theft. Its hackers stole 2 billion dollars in cryptocurrencies in 2025, including 1.5 billion from the Bybit hack alone, the largest digital asset theft ever recorded.

Satoshi Nakamoto wrote in 2008 that Bitcoin was a peer-to-peer electronic cash system, without intermediaries, without banks, without central control. The promise was to free ordinary citizens from the financial system that had betrayed them in the crisis. Sixteen years later, that promise has become the preferred infrastructure for three of the most repressive regimes on earth to evade the consequences of their actions.

This is not an accident. It is the structure. The decentralization that Bitcoin promised as liberation works exactly the same way for those who want to escape international justice. It does not distinguish between the dissident protecting savings from an authoritarian government and the authoritarian government financing terrorism. The technology is neutral. The consequences are not.

The final paradox is this: the bigger Bitcoin grows, the more it is watched. Market capitalization surpassed 2 trillion dollars in October 2025, drawing the attention of financial intelligence units worldwide. Governments that once struggled to track crypto transactions now use advanced analytics to link wallets to real identities. Europe’s DAC8 integrates crypto into automatic tax information exchange. The UK has introduced direct confiscation powers without arrest. The blockchain, which was supposed to be anonymous and untouchable, has become the most transparent ledger ever built: every transaction is permanent, public, and traceable.

Let us be clear. Bitcoin was meant to be the answer to 2008: an alternative financial system outside the control of banks and governments. The idea was that anyone, anywhere, could send and receive money without asking permission. An Iranian citizen protecting savings from devaluation. A Belarusian dissident receiving donations the regime could not block. These were the use cases Bitcoin supporters always cited. They were real. They still are. The problem is that the same technology works exactly the same way for the Iranian regime funding militias, for the Russian government buying oil around sanctions, for North Korea stealing billions to finance its nuclear program. Bitcoin does not discriminate. It cannot discriminate. That is both its strength and its fatal flaw.

Decentralization has freed someone. It just was not who Satoshi Nakamoto had in mind.
