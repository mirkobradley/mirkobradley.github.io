The End of Trust

There is a widespread misunderstanding about de-globalization. Many believe it means the end of world trade, that ships stop sailing and containers stop moving. That is not the case. Global trade is still at historic highs. Goods continue to flow. What has ended is not trade. It is trust.

Trust in the most concrete sense: a country’s willingness to depend on another for something essential. For decades the United States accepted dependence on China for manufactured goods. China accepted dependence on the United States for technology. Europe accepted dependence on Russia for gas. Everyone depended on Taiwan for chips. It was a system of mutual dependencies that worked as long as everyone trusted the others enough not to weaponize those dependencies.

Then something changed.

The first warning sign appeared in 2008, when the financial crisis revealed how fragile global supply chains really were. But the turning point came in 2018, when Trump imposed the first tariffs on China. A tariff is a tax on imports: if you buy a Chinese product, you pay an extra percentage to the American state. The effect is to make Chinese goods more expensive for American consumers and less competitive. Trump’s tariffs were not just an economic move. They were a political statement: America no longer trusts China as a trading partner.

Biden kept almost all of Trump’s tariffs. In his second term Trump raised them further, to levels approaching an embargo. The result is measurable: by the end of 2025, imports and exports between the US and China had fallen by more than 25 percent. In April 2025 China practically stopped buying American exports. Without the trade war that began in 2017, US exports to China would have been nearly 60 percent higher in 2025, about 90 billion dollars more every year.

But tariffs are only the surface. The deeper change is in the principle guiding economic decisions. For forty years the dominant principle was efficiency: buy from the cheapest place, produce where costs are lowest, optimize every link in the chain. Today the dominant principle is security: buy from the most reliable place, produce where you have control, never depend on anyone for what you cannot afford to lose. Efficiency and security are opposing goals. Maximizing one means sacrificing the other.

This shift has a name: friend-shoring. Instead of buying from the cheapest country, you buy from an ally. Instead of optimizing a single global supply chain, you build regional chains among countries that trust each other enough. The world is dividing into blocs: an American bloc, a Chinese bloc, and a Europe struggling to stay in the middle.

Covid accelerated everything. When Chinese factories closed in 2020 and supply chains seized up, every government understood how dangerous it was to rely on a single supplier for essential goods. Masks, medicines, chips, steel: suddenly it was obvious that the logic of efficiency had created systemic vulnerabilities no one had calculated. The pandemic turned de-globalization from a trend into explicit policy.

There is a historical precedent worth remembering, even if it is uncomfortable. At the beginning of the 20th century the world economy was highly integrated. International trade was at record levels. The great European powers were deeply interdependent. Many economists of the time argued that war between them would be economically suicidal and therefore impossible. In 1914 the First World War broke out. Today’s de-globalization does not necessarily lead to war, but those who believe economic interdependence guarantees peace should remember that this argument has already been disproven once, in the worst possible way.

China responded to American pressure with a move Friedman never foresaw: control over rare earths. Rare earths are a group of metals essential for chips, batteries, electric motors, and advanced weapons systems. China controls over 60 percent of global production and nearly 90 percent of processing. In the summer of 2025, in response to American tariffs, Beijing imposed export restrictions on rare earths to the United States. It was a clear warning: you can tax our goods, but you depend on us for the materials you need to build yours.

This is the new face of economic geopolitics. No longer armies clashing on battlefields, but supply chains used as levers of pressure. Whoever controls a critical material, a strategic port, or a key technology holds power measured not in military divisions but in economic dependencies.

The world has not de-globalized. It has re-globalized around distrust.
