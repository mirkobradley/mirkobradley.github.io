The World Was Flat. For a While

In 2005 Thomas Friedman, a New York Times columnist, published “The World Is Flat”. It sold four million copies, won the Financial Times Business Book of the Year, and became required reading in top MBA programs worldwide. The thesis was simple: technology had leveled the global playing field. Fiber optics, the internet, and software had demolished geographical barriers. An engineer in Bangalore could work for a Silicon Valley company. A factory in China could produce for an American consumer. Distance no longer mattered. The world was flat.

It was a powerful idea. It was also, to a large extent, wrong.

To understand why it was wrong, we must first understand why it seemed so right. Modern globalization emerged from three converging events at the end of the 1980s and early 1990s. The fall of the Berlin Wall in 1989 opened the markets of Eastern Europe. The collapse of the Soviet Union in 1991 eliminated the ideological alternative to capitalism. China’s entry into the WTO in 2001 integrated the world’s most populous country into global supply chains. In less than fifteen years, billions of people entered the world economy. The result was an unprecedented boom in international trade, which grew at an average of 6 percent per year between 1990 and 2008, twice the rate of global GDP.

The model worked like this: rich countries design, poor countries produce. America invents the iPhone, China assembles it. Germany designs machine tools, Eastern Europe builds them. Every country does what it does best, at the lowest possible cost. Adam Smith and David Ricardo had predicted it two centuries earlier. It is called comparative advantage: specialization creates efficiency, efficiency creates wealth, and wealth spreads. The logic was impeccable, and the accompanying ideology even more so.

Friedman went further and proposed what he called the “Dell Theory of Conflict Prevention”. No country integrated into global supply chains would ever go to war with another integrated country, because the economic cost would be unbearable. Trade would replace war, and peace would be guaranteed by economic interdependence. It was a seductive idea. In February 2022 Russia invaded Ukraine. It was fully integrated into the global economy. It exported gas, grain, and metals. It imported technology, consumer goods, and capital. The Dell Theory died live on television.

In reality Richard Florida had already answered Friedman in 2005 on the pages of The Atlantic. The title of his article was “The World Is Spiky”: the world is not flat, it is spiky. Economic power does not spread evenly thanks to technology. It concentrates in a few nodes: New York, London, Tokyo, Shanghai, San Francisco. Globalization did not level the playing field. It amplified the differences between those already at the top and those at the bottom. The data supported him then. It supports him even more today.

Globalization produced real growth. It lifted hundreds of millions out of poverty, especially in Asia. It lowered consumer prices for Western workers even as their wages stagnated. It created a global middle class that had never existed before. These are facts, not ideology.

But it also created systemic fragility. When every country does only one thing and depends on everyone else for everything else, a break in any part of the chain spreads everywhere. Covid proved it in 2020: Chinese factories closed, ports jammed, cars stopped being produced because of missing chips, hospitals ran out of masks because they were all made in a single industrial district in Shandong. Maximum efficiency is also maximum fragility. They are the same thing.

Friedman had described a world in which geopolitics had become irrelevant because it had been replaced by economics. He was wrong in the opposite direction from what he thought: geopolitics had not disappeared. It had returned more powerful than before, armed with the very tools that globalization had built. Trade as a weapon. Supply chains as leverage. Sanctions as a substitute for cannons, and semiconductors as the main battlefield.

The world was not flat. It was a network of dependencies that someone, sooner or later, would use as a weapon.
