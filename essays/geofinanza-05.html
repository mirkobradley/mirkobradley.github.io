The Weapons of the Chip War

Before discussing the weapons, we must clarify Taiwan’s status. Otherwise nothing that follows or the broader US-China relationship makes sense.

Taiwan is an island of 23 million people that has governed itself since 1949, when the Chinese Nationalist government fled there after losing the civil war to Mao’s communists. Since then Taiwan has had its own parliament, its own military, its own currency, and its own foreign policy. In every practical sense, Taiwan is an independent state. Yet it is not formally recognized as such, and this distinction lies at the heart of one of the most dangerous geopolitical ambiguities in the world.

China considers Taiwan a rebellious province that must be reunified with the mainland, by force if necessary. This position is non-negotiable for Beijing, as it is written into the Chinese constitution and repeated by every leader of the Communist Party. The United States officially follows the “One China” policy, acknowledging that there is only one China and taking note of Beijing’s claim that Taiwan is part of it. Formally, the US does not recognize Taiwan as a sovereign state and has no official diplomatic relations with it.

Yet the United States sells weapons to Taiwan, maintains relations through the American Institute in Taiwan (which functions as an embassy in all but name), and is bound by the Taiwan Relations Act of 1979, which commits it to provide Taiwan with the means to defend itself. Would the US intervene militarily if China attacked Taiwan? It does not say yes, but it also does not say no. This deliberate ambiguity is designed to deter China from attacking and Taiwan from formally declaring independence.

With TSMC the relationship has become even tighter. The United States convinced TSMC to build factories in Arizona for strategic reasons: in the event of a crisis in the Taiwan Strait, Washington wants guaranteed access to the world’s most advanced technology. TSMC founder Morris Chang has publicly stated that these factories will cost far more and be less efficient than those in Taiwan, but they are being built anyway because Washington asked and is paying for them.

This is the context in which the chip war must be understood. It is not a commercial war between companies. It is a strategic war between states, fought with economic and legal tools rather than military ones.

The first weapon is the Entity List. This is a blacklist managed by the US Bureau of Industry and Security. Companies placed on it cannot purchase American technology without a special license, which is rarely granted. Huawei was added in 2019 and has since been unable to buy chips from Qualcomm, software from Google, or equipment from Applied Materials. The result was immediate: Huawei lost its leadership in the smartphone market because it could no longer produce advanced chips. Today the list includes hundreds of Chinese companies, from chipmakers to military research labs.

The second weapon is the Foreign Direct Product Rule. This is the boldest and most controversial measure. It states that any product, even if manufactured outside the United States, falls under US jurisdiction if it uses American technology or software at any stage of its production or design. In practice, this allows the US to control what TSMC produces in Taiwan, because TSMC uses American software to design chips. This rule enabled the blocking of TSMC from producing chips for Huawei, even though TSMC is a Taiwanese company operating on Taiwanese soil. It is an unprecedented extraterritorial extension of American law.

The third weapon is the CHIPS Act, signed by Biden in August 2022. It allocates 52 billion dollars to incentivize chip production in the United States, but contains a clause more important than the subsidies: any company receiving funds cannot expand its production in China for ten years. TSMC, Samsung, and Intel accepted the money and the restriction. It is a way to use American public money as leverage to separate global supply chains.

The fourth weapon is multilateral diplomacy. The United States convinced the Netherlands not to sell ASML’s EUV machines to China. It convinced Japan to restrict exports of chip manufacturing equipment. It has built a Western technological bloc that isolates China from frontier technology. Every allied country that joins pays a real economic cost, since China is a massive market, but Washington has applied enough pressure to make strategic considerations prevail over commercial ones.

The system has structural limits. China has responded by trying to circumvent every restriction. Some Chinese companies have restructured to escape the Entity List. Nvidia has created specially calibrated versions of its chips to stay just below US control thresholds. China has increased purchases of legacy chips to build stockpiles and has accelerated investments in SMIC to reduce dependence on foreign technology.

The American strategy has a name: “small yard, high fence.” The idea is not to block everything, but to protect the most critical technologies with maximum rigor. The problem is that the yard keeps expanding every time China finds a way around the fence.

Taiwan sits at the center of all this. It is not just the factory of the world’s most advanced chips. It is living proof that technology and geopolitics have become inseparable. Its legal ambiguity, neither fully sovereign state nor Chinese province, is the most dangerous gray zone of the 21st century.
