OpenAI: The Linchpin Nobody Has Ever Taken Apart

“OpenAI is the linchpin. Anyone know who their auditor is?”

Michael Burry posted that on X and moved on. Nobody answered him because the answer is uncomfortable. Almost no one knows who audits OpenAI. Its certified financial statements do not exist in any form that serious investors would accept. The numbers in circulation come from journalistic leaks, third-party SEC filings, and projections shared with investors bound by NDAs.

We are talking about a company valued at 852 billion dollars with no publicly certified balance sheet.

The numbers that are known look like this. In 2025 OpenAI generated 13 billion in revenue and spent 22 billion to do it. Net loss: 9 billion. It spent a dollar and sixty-nine cents for every dollar earned. In the third quarter of 2025 alone it lost 12 billion in three months. The burn rate is projected to remain at 57 percent of revenue in 2026 and 2027. By 2028 operating losses are expected to reach 74 billion in a single year before a hypothetical pivot to profitability in 2029-2030. Cumulative losses through 2029 are estimated at 115 billion.

CFO Sarah Friar has stated that the company has healthy margins and could break even whenever it wants. Sam Altman has said the same thing to journalists over dinner. Neither has ever shown a balance sheet.

This opacity is not accidental. OpenAI is not a public company so it has no obligation to disclose anything to the public. It quietly shifted from non-profit to for-profit while raising hundreds of billions. Investors signed NDAs. The numbers that surface do so indirectly: a Microsoft SEC filing here, a document shared with investors there, an anonymous source at the Wall Street Journal. As one analyst who tried to reconcile the available data wrote: the numbers do not add up and nobody is required to explain why.

OpenAI is not just another startup. It is the linchpin of the entire system. Banks issuing debt on AI data centers use OpenAI as their benchmark for future demand. Fund managers build valuations on OpenAI models. OpenAI is the justification for the whole narrative: the reference model, the capability benchmark, the living proof that AI is worth what the market says it is worth. If OpenAI cracks there is no longer a center. And the center has no public financials.

Here is the simple truth: OpenAI does not generate enough money to cover its costs. It spends 1.69 dollars for every dollar it makes. It survives only because it keeps raising fresh private capital. The last round was 122 billion in March 2026. But every new round must be larger than the previous one because the losses are growing and will keep growing. At some point private capital will stop coming or it will come on terms that destroy the current valuation. Or OpenAI will go public and forced disclosure will reveal numbers the market is unwilling to accept.

This is not a prediction of fraud. It is arithmetic. A company that burns far more than it earns at an accelerating rate requires continuous external financing. If that financing slows or stops the company cuts operations, lays off staff, reduces compute. The models degrade or become more expensive. Demand falls. Revenue falls. The spiral tightens.

There is a precedent worth remembering. Enron was the seventh-largest company in America by market cap. It had a respected auditor, Arthur Andersen, certifying its books. It had analysts covering it, investment-grade ratings, and a powerful narrative about transforming the energy market. When the structure was exposed nothing remained. The difference with OpenAI is that Enron at least had public financials to falsify.

The real risk of an IPO according to those who have studied OpenAI’s financial structure is not that investors will refuse to buy. It is that going public would force a level of disclosure that would fundamentally reshape the loss trajectory the private markets have so far chosen to ignore.

They chose to ignore it. Not failed to see it. Ignored it.

The linchpin holds as long as nobody takes it apart.
