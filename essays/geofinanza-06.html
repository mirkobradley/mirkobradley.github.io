The Chinese Response: Changing Lanes

American sanctions were supposed to slow China down, and in part they have. But they also produced an effect Washington did not anticipate: they forced Chinese researchers and companies to find solutions that the Western system, comfortable with its technological advantage, was not even looking for.

The Chinese response is advancing on three simultaneous fronts. The first is doing more with what they have. The second is building what they lack. The third, and most ambitious, is changing the rules of the game.

The first front is SMIC. Semiconductor Manufacturing International Corporation is China’s leading chipmaker. It does not have access to ASML’s EUV machines (the 150-million-euro Dutch lithography printers needed for chips below 7 nanometers), but its engineers have found a way around the problem by using older DUV machines with multi-patterning techniques. The idea is simple: if you cannot achieve sufficient precision in a single pass, you make multiple overlapping passes to reach the same result. The process is slower, more expensive, and less efficient, but it works.

In 2023 Huawei launched the Mate 60 Pro smartphone with the Kirin 9000S chip, produced by SMIC at 7 nanometers without EUV. It was proof that the American technological blockade had limits. In 2026 SMIC is doubling its 7-nanometer production capacity and has begun testing 5-nanometer processes. Huawei, through its HiSilicon division, is producing the Ascend series of AI chips and aims for 1.6 million units in 2026. Independent tests show that Huawei’s Ascend chips are competitive with the restricted versions of Nvidia chips available in China.

The second front is building an independent supply chain. In 2014 China created the Big Fund, a state fund to finance semiconductor development. Since then it has invested over 150 billion dollars. The results are visible. In 2020 China controlled about 3 percent of the global 12-inch silicon wafer market. By 2025 that share had risen to 28 percent and may reach 32 percent in 2026. Chinese companies in materials, equipment, and components are systematically reducing dependence on foreign sources. Not enough yet, but the direction is clear.

The third front is the most surprising. In March 2025 a research team at Peking University led by Professor Peng Hailin published in Nature the results of ten years of work on a new class of transistors that do not use silicon. To understand the importance of this, consider how a traditional transistor works.

A transistor is a microscopic switch: open, it blocks the flow of electrons; closed, it lets them pass. A modern chip contains billions of these switches. The smaller they are, the more you can fit on a given area, and the more powerful the chip becomes. For decades the technological race was about making silicon transistors smaller and smaller. The problem is that silicon has a physical limit: when transistors shrink below a certain size, electrons begin to behave unpredictably and “leak” through the barriers meant to contain them. This quantum phenomenon is called tunneling and cannot be eliminated, only managed.

Peng’s team used an entirely different material: a bismuth-based compound called Bi&sub2;O&sub2;Se. Unlike silicon, which is a three-dimensional material, Bi&sub2;O&sub2;Se is a two-dimensional material organized in atomically thin layers, like stacked sheets. This atomic sheet structure allows much more precise control of electron flow than silicon, which begins to lose its electrical properties at very small scales. The flat atomic structure lets electrons move with very little resistance. As Peng himself described it, “like water flowing through a smooth pipe.” The measured result: Peng’s transistor operates 40 percent faster than the best 3-nanometer chips from TSMC and Intel, while consuming 10 percent less energy. In tests against the best devices from Intel, TSMC, Samsung, and the IMEC center in Belgium, the Chinese transistor won in every condition.

Peng described his discovery with a precise metaphor: “If innovations based on existing materials are a shortcut, developing transistors based on 2D materials is like changing lanes.” It is not an incremental improvement on silicon. It is an entirely different path that bypasses the bottleneck where China is stuck, born, as Peng acknowledged, from the necessity imposed by sanctions.

It must be said clearly: Peng’s transistor exists in the laboratory, not in the factory. Moving from prototype to industrial production of billions of transistors per chip is a process that takes years, perhaps decades. But the same was said about 7-nanometer production without EUV that SMIC demonstrated in 2023. The sanctions have created extraordinary incentives for Chinese innovation that may be opening new roads.

There is a deeper logic here worth stating. The United States built its technological advantage in an open system, where the free circulation of ideas, talent, and capital accelerated innovation. When they began building walls, they also reduced their ability to absorb the best global ideas. China, shut out of the system, has had to invent its own, and it is not clear that it is worse.

The sanctions stopped China from buying American technology, but they did not stop it from inventing its own.
