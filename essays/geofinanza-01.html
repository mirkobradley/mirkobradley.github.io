TSMC: The Invisible Linchpin of the World

There is a company you have probably never heard of that makes the components without which your smartphone, your computer, your car, and the data centers powering the AI you use every day would not function. Its name is TSMC, Taiwan Semiconductor Manufacturing Company. Based in Hsinchu, Taiwan, it controls 70 percent of the global market for advanced chip manufacturing. It produces semiconductors for Nvidia, Apple, Qualcomm, and Broadcom. Without TSMC, Nvidia cannot make the chips that run ChatGPT.

To understand what semiconductors are and why they matter, consider this: a chip is a piece of silicon the size of a postage stamp containing billions of transistors, microscopic switches that process information at the speed of light. The more transistors you can pack onto that stamp, the more powerful the chip. The entire technological challenge lies in extreme miniaturization, measured in nanometers, one billionth of a meter. The most advanced chips today are made at 3 nanometers. Only TSMC and Samsung can do it, and only TSMC does it reliably at industrial scale.

The global semiconductor supply chain is one of the most complex constructions humanity has ever built. Chips are designed in the United States, silicon crystals grown in Japan, wafers cut in South Korea, fabrication completed in Taiwan, and final products assembled in China. Each stage requires specialized equipment, unique expertise, and decades of investment. It cannot be improvised or replicated quickly. The bottleneck of the entire chain is Taiwan.

The numbers explain this dependence. Over 60 percent of the world’s semiconductors and nearly 90 percent of the most advanced chips are produced on an island of just 36,000 square kilometers. In 2024 TSMC’s share of the contract manufacturing market rose to 64 percent. Its closest competitor, Samsung, sits at 11 percent. Economists estimate that a blockade of Taiwan would cause 2.7 trillion dollars in global economic losses in the first year alone, immediately halting 90 percent of advanced chip production.

This concentration did not happen by accident. TSMC was founded in 1987 through a deliberate strategic decision by the Taiwanese government: instead of competing with Intel and AMD in chip design, Taiwan would specialize in manufacturing for third parties. This is the foundry model: do not invent the product, build it better than anyone else. Sustained by massive investment, decades of accumulated know-how, and one of the world’s most skilled engineering bases, this specialization created a competitive advantage that no one has been able to replicate. Even with 200 billion dollars in American and European subsidies, the technological gap with Taiwan will remain significant for at least another decade.

This concentration has created what geopolitics experts call the “silicon shield.” Taiwan remains safe as long as it produces chips that everyone needs. A Chinese attack on Taiwan would not be just a military problem, it would be global economic suicide. China itself depends on Taiwan for the chips that power its industry. Destroying TSMC would mean destroying the world economy, including China’s.

But the silicon shield is also a trap. Taiwan has become so indispensable that it is now the most coveted target on the planet. The United States wants to bring advanced chip production back to American soil, not for economic reasons but for national security. China wants Taiwan, largely because Taiwan is TSMC, even as TSMC builds new factories in Arizona, Japan, and Germany, not out of free market choice but because governments are paying for it.

TSMC’s founder, Morris Chang, has said that globalization is “almost dead” and that chip production cannot be separated from geopolitics. He is right. TSMC is no longer just a company. It is the critical infrastructure around which the rivalry between the two superpowers of the 21st century is organized.

In Anatomy of a Catastrophe we dismantle the black box of AI finance piece by piece. OpenAI has so far been the opaque linchpin. TSMC is the physical one. Without it the entire machine stops, and it sits on an island contested by two superpowers.

The most advanced chip in the world is three nanometers wide and weighs less than a gram. The fate of the world depends on how many we produce and who controls that production.
