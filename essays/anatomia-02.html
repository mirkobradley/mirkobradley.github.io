When the Buyer Is Also the Seller

Nvidia is pouring $100 billion into OpenAI. OpenAI takes a stake in AMD. Microsoft is both a major shareholder in OpenAI and its biggest customer through CoreWeave. CoreWeave is backed by Nvidia. Microsoft accounts for nearly 20% of Nvidia’s revenue. Nvidia has promised to buy all of CoreWeave’s unused capacity until 2032.

Welcome to the largest market in the world, where the seller is the customer, the customer is the shareholder, and the shareholder is the supplier. A perfectly self-financed, self-valued, and self-validated system. More mirror than market.

Michael Burry is the hedge fund manager who made hundreds of millions betting against subprime mortgages in 2006. His story became the film “The Big Short.” He has seen this movie before. Today he is short Nvidia and posting on X: “The final question is ridiculously small. Almost all customers are financed by their dealers.” Then he adds: “OpenAI is the linchpin. Anyone know who their auditor is?”

That final question is exactly where the problem sits.

A technology creates real value when someone outside the system that produces it actually uses it to create something productive and pays with money earned elsewhere. The internet did that, with massive distortions but real underlying demand. Generative AI? Not so much. Ninety percent of American companies report zero measurable impact on productivity or revenue. They buy subscriptions and APIs mostly to avoid looking outdated to investors. The demand is defensive and reputational. It is nowhere near big enough to justify a trillion dollars in infrastructure.

So why does everyone believe? Because cognitive redundancy does not require AI to actually work. It only requires that everyone uses the same tools to think. A fund manager using AI models to evaluate AI companies, a CEO using ChatGPT to write strategy for investors who then use ChatGPT to analyze it. Nobody is getting more productive. They are simply synchronizing their thinking on the same signal. The homogenization of thought comes before and regardless of real economic impact. That is precisely why it is so dangerous.

The “new paradigm” is a financial narrative first, a technological one second. It exists to justify valuations that would otherwise be unsustainable. Once all decision-makers adopt the same narrative, it becomes self-fulfilling, truth be damned. Companies invest in AI not because it delivers value, but because not doing so signals backwardness. Investors reward those who invest because everyone else is doing the same. It is an equilibrium built on reciprocal expectations, not fundamentals.

There is one technical detail that makes the whole thing far more fragile than it appears. The real economic life of a GPU is roughly one year. A data center full of H100s in 2024 is already obsolete in 2025. Depreciation schedules in the books are too long, collateral values are overstated, and cash flows rest on assumptions that collapse with every new architecture release. The same banks issuing the SRTs we discussed in Anatomy 01 are now using AI models to value AI collateral with a twelve-month shelf life.

When the mirror breaks, who pays? The pension funds that bought AI debt thinking they were diversifying. The workers replaced by systems that never delivered the promised productivity. And everyone else, through the inevitable socialization of losses once the public bailout arrives, exactly like 2008.

The system does not collapse because AI does not work. It collapses because everyone used the exact same cognitive model to decide to bet on it. When the narrative cracks, it cracks simultaneously for all, with the very same tools that built it.

The mirror never warns before it shatters.
