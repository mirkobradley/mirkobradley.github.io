OpenAI: The Moment of Truth

OpenAI is preparing its filing for an initial public offering. The debut is scheduled for the fourth quarter of 2026. The private valuation has jumped from 500 billion dollars in October 2025 to 852 billion at the beginning of 2026. The declared target is 1 trillion.

In Anatomy 03 we asked Burry’s question: does anyone know who audits OpenAI? In a few months that question will receive a mandatory answer. Going public in the United States means filing an S-1 document with the SEC. The S-1 is not a promotional brochure. It is a legal document that requires audited financial statements by independent auditors, detailed explanations of how the company makes and spends money, disclosure of material risks, related-party transactions, and executive compensation. Everything public. Everything verifiable. Everything subject to civil and criminal liability.

OpenAI has never published anything like this. The numbers in circulation, the 13 billion in revenue for 2025, the 22 billion in costs, the 9 billion net loss, come from journalistic leaks, analyst estimates, and statements made by the CFO on CNBC. These figures are not certified and have never been verified by any independent auditor. The private market accepted these conditions because the funding rounds were backed by narrative, not fundamentals. The public market does not work that way.

The numbers the S-1 will have to certify look like this: a net loss of 9 billion in 2025, a burn rate of 57 percent of revenue in 2026 and 2027, operating losses projected to reach 74 billion in a single year in 2028, and cumulative losses estimated at 115 billion through 2029. HSBC estimates additional capital needs exceeding 207 billion by 2030. The most revealing figure: the 122 billion private round in March 2026 alone was 2.6 times larger than the entire US IPO market in 2025.

The structural problem is that the IPO is not a strategic choice. It is a financial survival necessity. Estimated cumulative compute spending through 2030 stands at 600 billion dollars. The private market has already absorbed more than is historically sustainable. The IPO would serve as a partial financing step, not a structural solution. In other words, OpenAI is going public not because it is ready, but because it needs money that the private market can no longer provide on its own.

There is one obstacle no press release mentions. OpenAI’s corporate structure is still transitioning from a non-profit to a public benefit corporation, the legal form required for listing. This transition is not yet complete, and open funding rounds must be closed before the filing. The rewritten partnership with Microsoft in April 2026 made the license on OpenAI’s code non-exclusive. Any of these elements can delay or complicate the process, and every delay means raising more capital in the meantime.

The comparison with SpaceX is instructive. SpaceX filed its S-1 confidentially on April 1 2026 and made it public on May 20. Its 2025 audited financials show 18.67 billion in verified revenue. Real numbers. For OpenAI, nothing comparable exists yet. When the S-1 arrives, the market will see for the first time the real numbers of a company valued by its private investors at nearly the same level as Meta and almost double Uber at the time of its IPO.

The real risk is not that the numbers are fake. It is that they are true. The loss trajectory may be exactly what external estimates suggest, the capital needs may be exactly what HSBC calculated, and the public market, unlike the private one, may not be willing to accept it without a credible path to profitability.

Burry asked who audits OpenAI. In a few months we will all know, and we will also know what they found.

The linchpin is about to be dismantled in public.
