The Chip Is the New Oil

In the 20th century oil was the resource that determined the fate of nations. Whoever controlled it dominated industry, transportation, and the military. Wars were fought to secure it and alliances were built around it. Countries without oil were dependent on those who had it. In the 21st century that resource is the semiconductor. Every year China spends as much importing chips as it does importing oil. This is not a coincidence. It is a measure of how essential chips have become.

To understand why semiconductors have become so strategic, consider what they do. A semiconductor is the brain of every electronic device: the smartphone, the computer, the modern car, the cruise missile, facial recognition systems, and artificial intelligence models. Without advanced chips you cannot build modern weapons, train frontier AI models, or run 5G networks. Whoever controls the production of advanced chips controls the future of military, economic, and technological power. This is not an exaggeration. It is why the United States and China are fighting the most important battle of our time on this exact ground.

The chip war officially began in 2022, when the Biden administration introduced the strictest export controls ever imposed on technology. The logic is simple: if China cannot buy advanced American chips, it cannot build advanced weapons or train frontier AI models. The controls hit three levels: finished chips, the equipment to make them, and the software to design them. It is like blocking the factory, the machinery, and the blueprints at the same time.

The main target is Nvidia. Nvidia produces the GPU chips that power artificial intelligence. Without them you cannot train large language models like ChatGPT, run AI data centers, or develop automated targeting systems for military use. Since 2022 the United States has progressively banned the sale of Nvidia’s most powerful chips to China. Every time Nvidia tried to circumvent the ban by creating a slightly less powerful version for the Chinese market, the US government lowered the threshold further. It is an endless chase.

The paradox of this chase became clear in March 2026. At Nvidia’s GTC conference, CEO Jensen Huang announced orders for over 400,000 H200 chips from major Chinese companies: ByteDance, Alibaba, Tencent. But Nvidia has not collected a single dollar from these sales. The chips have been produced and the orders confirmed, yet delivery depends on permission from the Chinese government. Nvidia has the chips, the Chinese customers want them, but the real game is being played between Washington and Beijing, not between seller and buyer.

On the Chinese side the response has two parts. The first is immediate retaliation: in the summer of 2025 Beijing imposed restrictions on the export of rare earths, the critical metals needed to produce chips, batteries, and advanced weapons. The second is the long-term plan: building an independent supply chain. SMIC, China’s leading chipmaker, has made real progress. It has developed manufacturing processes approaching 5 nanometers without using Dutch ASML lithography equipment, which the United States has banned from export to China. It is not yet at TSMC’s level, but the gap is narrowing.

However, there is a structural limit China still cannot overcome. Producing advanced chips requires equipment that only a few countries can build. The most important machine is called EUV (Extreme Ultraviolet Lithography): a giant printer that etches transistors onto silicon using ultraviolet light. Only one company in the world makes it: the Dutch firm ASML. Each EUV machine costs about 150 million euros and requires components from over 5,000 suppliers worldwide. The United States convinced the Netherlands not to sell these machines to China. Without EUV, China cannot reliably produce chips below 7 nanometers at industrial scale.

The result is an increasingly deep technological bifurcation. On one side the American bloc with TSMC, Samsung, Nvidia, ASML, and Intel. On the other the Chinese bloc with SMIC, Huawei, and a supply chain still partially dependent on the outside but developing rapidly. In the middle are countries trying to remain neutral, such as India, Vietnam, and Mexico, which are becoming the new destinations for reshoring.

The cost of all this is enormous. Building a new advanced chip factory in 2026 costs between 35 and 43 billion dollars, 30 percent more than a few years ago. The United States has allocated 52 billion through the CHIPS Act. Europe has allocated 43 billion with its European Chips Act. China has invested over 150 billion in the last ten years. None of these investments are driven by market logic. They are all driven by national security logic. This is the kind of spending you make when preparing for war, not when optimizing a business.

In the 20th century wars were fought for oil. In the 21st century they are fought for chips. The difference is that this war is currently being waged with tariffs, bans, and subsidies instead of armies. For now.
