Inside the Black Box · BB·01

Introduction: Why I Am Writing This Series

There is a great deal of noise around artificial intelligence at the moment. Most of it comes from two directions that I find equally unsatisfying: on one side uncritical enthusiasm, on the other a diffuse and undefined fear. What these two positions share, despite their opposite tone, is the same underlying mistake. Both anthropomorphise, projecting intentions, emotions and agency onto systems that are, in their structure, purely mathematical operations on probability distributions. The problem itself lies in the mathematical architecture of the frame.

Looking inside the “black box” means first understanding what it is made of. Current language models are neural networks trained on enormous amounts of text and, through this training, they learn to predict which word or token (the minimal units of language) follows another by optimising a mathematical function over billions of parameters. AI can be thought of as a vast mathematical system, with various branches (models), which in itself has no objectives, no persistent internal states and no internal memory across conversations. What these models produce is statistically coherent and often surprisingly useful, but it emerges from a process of compressing and reconstructing the structure of human language, not from anything resembling understanding in the sense in which we use the term. To grasp the real limits of these models, we need to free our thinking from science fiction. It is best to set aside the idea that AI is dangerous because it wants to dominate the world. If anything, it is problematic because it hallucinates, because it can amplify the biases present in its training data, because it does not distinguish what it knows from what it does not know, and because its rapid diffusion into decision-making systems can produce effects in society that no single model ever intended.

I am writing this series because I think the most useful thing one can do right now is to look carefully at what is actually inside the “black box”, not metaphorically but structurally. I intend to clarify what kind of mathematics drives these systems, where their real limits lie, and how ethical questions appear once the science fiction layer is removed. The public debate on AI, somewhere between the technical and the vaguely philosophical, also oscillates between those who say it is just statistics, therefore nothing to worry about, and those who say it is about to become conscious and dangerous. Both positions rely on the same mistaken categories: intelligence, intentions, choices, emotions. Understanding why those categories are mistaken is the starting point.
