What a Model of History Can Predict

History is unpredictable. This is the common conviction, and it is partly true. No model can tell you that the Roman Empire will fall symbolically on September 4th, 476, that the USSR will dissolve in 1991, or that a financial crisis will erupt in the autumn of 2008. Individual events depend on irreducible contingencies such as the choice of a single person at a critical moment, a pandemic, or an unforeseen discovery. No mathematics removes this contingency.

But “history is unpredictable” does not mean that all of history is equally unpredictable. There is a precise and often overlooked difference between predicting an event and predicting the structure that makes it possible.

A seismograph does not predict earthquakes. It measures the accumulation of tension in the earth’s crust, distinguishes high-tension zones from stable ones, and identifies structurally fragile systems. It does not know when the fault will give way, nor how violent the release will be. Yet its usefulness is real and precise, because it informs decisions under conditions of structural uncertainty. A historical system works in the same way. One can identify zones of tension, measure accumulated fragility, and locate the points where the system is maximally sensitive to perturbations. This is not a small thing. It is exactly the kind of knowledge that is missing when collapses arrive as surprises.

Isaac Asimov had intuited something similar. The psychohistory imagined in the Foundation cycle is one of the most fertile epistemic intuitions in the science fiction of the twentieth century: a branch of mathematics capable of predicting the behavior of human masses on century-long timescales, ignoring individual contingency in order to capture the statistical laws of the collective. The intuition was right, at least in part. The limit was elsewhere.

Asimov himself sensed it. In the fiction, psychohistory works only on the condition that the masses do not know they are being governed by it. The moment the model becomes public, its perturbing effect invalidates it. This narrative device hides a real problem, namely that a formal model of history cannot ignore its own effect on the system it describes. When a model becomes known, believed, and acted upon by historical agents, it enters the system it describes and alters its trajectory. Asimov solved this with secrecy. The scientific solution requires something more complex.

Before getting there, it is worth surveying the existing formal models of history, because each captures something real and each stops short of the decisive point.

Peter Turchin, with cliodynamics, has shown that historical cycles of instability can be modeled with differential equations and empirically falsified. Demographic-fiscal cycles, elite overproduction, and patterns of political violence follow identifiable structures. It is the most rigorous methodological contribution in the field. The limit is that the model is external to the system it describes: as in physics, the observer does not alter the phenomenon. In history, this assumption does not hold.

George Soros identified and analyzed reflexivity in financial markets with notable conceptual precision: the expectations of agents do not merely reflect fundamentals, they modify them. Soros’s boom-bust cycle is a qualitative description of a real phenomenon. The limit is that Soros does not extend reflexivity to history in a broader sense and lacks a formal structure that would allow falsifiable predictions outside financial markets.

Immanuel Wallerstein treated history as a global system with long-term structures, and his analysis of hegemonic transitions captures something real. The limit is structuralist determinism. In his model, historical trajectories are determined by the structure of the world-system and agents are essentially epiphenomena. There is no space for stochastic contingency or reflexivity.

Nassim Taleb forcefully highlighted the fragility of homogeneous systems and the systematic underestimation of extreme events. The black swan is a description of the effect of historical bifurcations from the perspective of someone who lacks a model of structural instability. The limit is a generalized skepticism toward formal modeling: Taleb prefers robustness strategies to predictions. It is a defensible position, but it gives up something important.

René Thom provided the mathematical vocabulary to describe bifurcations as stable geometric structures of state space. The elementary catastrophes are local prototypes of the critical transitions recognizable in history. The contribution is geometric and conceptual; his catastrophe theory applied to historical systems lacked a stochastic and reflexive component.

Ibn Khaldun, in the fourteenth century, identified elite cohesion as the key variable in the stability and collapse of dynasties. His dynastic cycle is the first systematic formulation of an idea that all later models had to reinvent. He lacked mathematical formalization, but the intuition was correct.

The common limit of all these models is a single one: none incorporates its own effect on the system it describes. All treat the model as external to history, as a tool that observes without participating. But in real history, models are known, disseminated, believed, acted upon. They enter the system. They modify it. And this modification is itself historical.

This is the starting point of the framework I will present in this series. The historical system I propose, S = (H, M, Φ, α), includes a variable that all other models ignore: α(t), the degree to which the model is known, believed, and acted upon by the agents of the system. When α is high, the model no longer describes history from the outside, because it is part of it. And this changes everything.

In the posts that follow, I will build this framework piece by piece. The guiding question will remain the same: what it can and cannot predict. The answer, as you will see, is more precise and more interesting than common conviction suggests.
