Why every model of the mind feels wrong to me

When you Google "how the mind works", you mostly find three kinds of answers.

The first kind says the mind is the brain. Neurons, synapses, cortical areas. Every thought has a neural correlate, every emotion has a location. It's a clean, measurable, scientific model. The problem is that it doesn't explain anything you actually care about. It can tell you where the brain lights up when you're afraid, but it can't tell you why that fear has been paralysing you for twenty years and not your brother.

The second kind says the mind is experience. Phenomenology, consciousness, subjective life. It's a rich, nuanced model, close to what you actually feel. The problem is that it can't be formalised. You can describe experience endlessly, but you can't measure it, you can't compare it, you can't predict anything.

The third kind says the mind is a dynamical system. Attractors, bifurcations, complexity. It's the most sophisticated model, and the closest to where I want to go. The problem is that it stops halfway: it uses the mathematics of complex systems to describe the brain, then goes back to explaining everything in terms of subject, experience, lived world. Dynamics comes in through the door, the subject sneaks back in through the window.

There is a problem that all these approaches share, beyond their differences. None of them quantifies. They describe, interpret, narrate. But without formalisation there is no falsifiability, and without falsifiability there is no science in the strong sense of the word. We see the result every day: dozens of models of the mind contradicting each other, each with its own concepts, its own language, its own arguments. Not because researchers are incompetent, but because in the absence of a shared formal structure there is no criterion for deciding who is right.

None of these models satisfies me. Not because they are wrong in the trivial sense. Each of them captures something real. The problem is that they all share the same implicit assumption, and that assumption blocks them all in the same way.

The assumption is this: the mind belongs to someone.

It sounds obvious. It even sounds tautological. But it is precisely this "obvious" that prevents us from building a real theory. As long as the mind is thought of as the property of a subject, it cannot become the object of a formal theory. Because the subject is not measurable, not comparable, not formalizable. It is the blind spot of every model.

The question I asked myself is simple: what happens if we remove the subject from the equation? We don't deny it. The subject exists, experience exists. But we set them aside as a starting point, and try to build a theory of mind that does not presuppose them.

What emerges is something unexpected. The mind, seen without the subject, is neither an object nor a process nor a system. It is a form. A dynamic configuration that stabilises, deforms, migrates, collapses. A structure with measurable properties, that evolves according to describable laws, that allows for precise predictions.

In the next posts we'll build this idea piece by piece. Without presupposing anything, without taking anything for granted. Always starting from a concrete problem, and only bringing in the mathematics once the ground has been prepared.

The subject will return. But as an emergence, not as a foundation.
