Can Qualia Be Mathematised?

There is an objection that always returns when I talk about mathematizing the mind. It comes especially from those who work with subjective experience, such as psychoanalysts, phenomenologists, and clinicians. It usually sounds like this: “Fine, mathematics can describe neurons, behaviours, trajectories. But the pain I feel, the beauty I perceive, the anguish that wakes me at night… these things cannot be measured. They belong to a domain that mathematics cannot reach.”

It is a serious objection. I do not dismiss it. Yet it contains a presupposition worth examining.

The presupposition is that mathematizing something means establishing a perfect equivalence between mathematics and natural language. If that were the goal, their resistance would be justified. The actual task is different: to capture with precision certain systemic dynamics that natural language grasps only vaguely and intuitively.

What the mathematical model describes, measures, and predicts is the geometry of the systemic space that natural language labels as “experience”.

Let’s try an example. When you feel intense pain, something changes in your space of possibilities. Certain thoughts become inaccessible, certain movements impossible. Plans for the future may vanish. This is not a metaphor: your space of compatible configurations — what in my mathematical model I call space C — contracts. Some directions close, some trajectories disappear, some attractors become unreachable.

This contraction is measurable. I am not measuring “how much it hurts” in the subjective sense of ordinary language; I am measuring how much variety has been lost, in which direction, and at what rate. What I call the total morphological cost, Utot, records exactly this contraction.

In simple terms: what we call pain is not something added to the deformation of the system’s space of possibilities, but that very deformation seen from within. The system does not “feel” pain as an event separate from its structure; it registers it because its own form changes, and that change orients its response. Pain is the name we give to this process, not a hidden mystery behind it.

Common sense assumes that inside us there is someone who “observes” internal states and records them: an observer separate from the structure, a self behind experience that manages it. The model removes this presupposition. There is no little homunculus at the centre of the system, no internal observer standing apart. There is only the form reacting to itself: the awareness of pain is the effect of the system’s capacity to register and respond to its own deformation. All of this is perfectly mathematizable.

There is another related objection, perhaps even more radical: mathematics does not capture nuances. The inner world is made of states that shade into one another, emotions that blend, experiences without sharp boundaries. A mathematics built on numbers and discrete categories cannot, by definition, capture this fluidity.

This presupposition is also mistaken. Topology — the branch of mathematics that studies the properties of spaces and their continuous deformations — is precisely the mathematics of nuance. It does not work with sharp boundaries but with gradual transformations, with spaces that deform without breaking, with structures that change shape while preserving coherence. It is the natural tool for describing what slides, bends, narrows, or expands without discontinuity.

Other branches of mathematics extend this capacity even further. Category theory describes relations and transformations between structures without fixing rigid boundaries between them. Differential geometry studies how spaces vary locally, capturing nuances that depend on viewpoint and context. Probability theory and stochastic processes describe states that are never definitive but oscillate with continuous weights. Combined, these tools reach a descriptive granularity that natural language does not possess.

The same applies to affectivity. In the model, the system’s dynamics follow the slope of the landscape of possibilities. Imagine the mind as a hilly terrain: the system moves naturally downhill, like a drop of water, driven by the local slope. This slope is what mathematicians call a gradient. Affectivity is therefore not a state but a direction. Valence — positive or negative — corresponds to the orientation of the slope: the system descends toward lower-cost configurations or is pushed uphill toward higher-cost ones. Intensity corresponds to how steep that slope is.

An intense emotion is a steep slope: the system is strongly driven toward a specific direction. A flat emotion is an almost level terrain: the system drifts without a dominant direction. Anguish is a contradictory landscape, where slopes pull in directions that do not converge.

All of this is comparable, predictable, and formalizable. This leads to the most important question: what is the point of mathematizing what is commonly called the “inner world”?

A purely intuitive approach to the mind, however rich, works by trial and error. Traditional clinical intervention modifies the system in an artisanal way. For example, the therapist intuits where a rigidity has formed, where the space of possibilities has narrowed, and attempts to intervene through language, relationship, interpretation. It often works. Yet without a formal map of the territory, the work proceeds in the dark. We remain at the level of honest craftsmanship.

Knowing the geometry of the system’s space of possibilities changes the nature of intervention. One no longer intervenes by trial, but with topological precision. One knows where the system’s form has contracted, in which direction it is moving, and which attractors have become too deep to be abandoned spontaneously. Intervention becomes a targeted modification of the structure of constraints, not a hopeful conversation in the dark.

As AI advances, this direction becomes even more concrete. The idea of a formal “psychosurgery” is not absurd: interventions that reshape the configuration of a system with the same precision with which we now intervene on physical structures. This is not about manipulating experience, but about modifying the geometry that otherwise makes certain experiences inevitable. It is, in essence, what psychotherapy aims to do. The difference is that with a formal model, one can do it knowing exactly what is being touched.
