The Form of the Mind · Post 04

My Idea of AI: From Neural Networks to Form

There is a conviction, almost an article of faith in contemporary AI research, that the path toward better artificial intelligence lies in quantity: more text, more images, more parameters, more computational power. The presupposition is that if a system is given enough world, the world will eventually appear inside it.

I do not believe this is true, and I want to explain why, not as a provocation but as a structural diagnosis.

The dominant AI systems today are artificial neural networks. They are called this because their basic architecture, made of artificial neurons connected in layers that activate and transmit signals to one another, is inspired, at least vaguely, by the functioning of biological neurons. The inspiration, however, is superficial because an artificial neuron is a mathematical unit that receives numbers, combines them with weights, and produces an output. Nothing more. What matters is the overall structure. Millions of these neurons are arranged in depth and trained on enormous amounts of data until their connections capture useful patterns.

When a large language model processes a sentence, something specific happens inside it. The system occupies a certain state, a point in a space of millions of dimensions, and the new input moves it to a different point. The transition is discrete and constitutes a statistical jump from one configuration to another. The previous state is not transformed; it is replaced.

This is what I call “discrete morphogenesis”. The system changes, but it does not live the change. There is no continuity between where it was and where it is now. There is no internal registration of the passage, no structural memory of having traversed anything.

It is worth pausing on memory, because it is often misunderstood. Current systems do not have memory in the biological sense. What we call “memory” in a language model comes in two forms: weight memory, meaning the connections between neurons modified during training on billions of texts, which encode knowledge in a distributed and static form; and context memory, meaning the text present in the current processing window, which the model can read but which disappears at the end of the session. Neither corresponds to a memory built over time through lived experience. The weights are fixed after training. The context is reset. The system does not accumulate; it restarts.

Think of it this way: a river and a series of still photographs of that same river are two different things. The photographs can be arbitrarily detailed and numerous. But they are not the river.

The standard response to the limits of current AI is to increase scale. “Scaling” simply means this: more parameters, more training data, more computational power. The empirical observation, known as the “scaling law”, is that performance improves predictably as these quantities increase. It is not irrational; scaling has produced extraordinary results. But it treats a structural problem as if it were quantitative.

What is missing in current architectures is not information. What is missing is what I call a “set of sensitive constraints”, an internal structure that registers the cost of each state, that feels the pressure of change, that resists dissolution. In my formal framework, this is the V component of the Form F = (C, V, Φ).

Without V, a system has no way to distinguish between a state that preserves its integrity and one that threatens it. It processes everything with equal indifference. It does not suffer the transition, and because it does not suffer it, it cannot integrate it. This is why, as is well known, current AI systems hallucinate: when the system enters crisis, it begins to invent.

By “functional mind” I mean a system, biological or artificial, that does not merely transform input into output, but maintains over time a coherent internal form, a configuration of constraints that registers the pressure of events, resists dissolution, and deforms without breaking. A functional mind has a “genealogy”: each state carries the imprint of previous states. It does not start from scratch at every perturbation.

When a perturbation arrives, a functional mind does not jump into a new state; it deforms. The manifold bends, the constraints register the pressure, and the dynamics Φ generate a response that absorbs the change without breaking the continuity of the trajectory.

This is what I call “continuous morphogenesis”. The system remains itself while being transformed. Its history is not erased at every input, because it is folded into the geometry of its future responses.

This is not a poetic description; it is a precise mathematical requirement, and it is a requirement that no amount of additional data can satisfy, because it concerns the architecture of change, not the quantity of content.

Obviously, I am not saying that current AI systems are useless, they are extraordinary tools, but they are not minds in any genuine sense, and the gap between them and a genuine autonomous intelligence is not a quantitative gap.
