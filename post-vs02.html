The Topology of Technological Power

May 2026

The approach to technological power remains traditional, representing it as a static and pyramidal structure. Technology, by contrast, has assumed a dynamic and horizontal form that adapts continuously to perturbations. We should not look at the brands but at the infrastructure that sustains them.

The Form of Power

When we speak of technological power, the ordinary question is: who holds it? Who controls Google, who owns Amazon, who sits on Meta's board. It is a legitimate question yet an insufficient one. It describes the distribution of power rather than its form, and the form is what matters, because the form determines how power propagates, where it concentrates and where it becomes vulnerable.

Topology is the mathematics of form. It studies relations, connections and the global structure of a system independently of its local dimensions. A railway network and a neural network may share the same topology while being physically incomparable. What topology captures is the architecture of relations: who is connected to whom, with what density and through how many intermediaries.

Applied to technological power, the topological question is not who holds power but what shape the network has through which power is exercised. The answer reveals something ordinary analysis does not see.

The Scale-Free Network

Networks of technological power have a multiform structure. The distribution of connections across nodes is not homogeneous, because some nodes have an enormous number of connections while most have very few. In this sense we speak of scale-free networks, where the distribution of connections follows a power law.

This is a structural property with precise consequences. In a scale-free network, nodes with many connections, the hubs, are qualitatively different, and removing them would fragment the network into isolated components, while removing peripheral nodes produces no meaningful effect on the global structure.

Google is a hub in this topological sense: removing it from the global information network would produce a fragmentation no other node could compensate for. The same holds for Amazon in logistics, for AWS in computational infrastructure, for GitHub in the software ecosystem. These nodes are powerful because the topology of the network renders them structurally irreplaceable.

The Difference from Earlier Forms of Power

The power of the state, the church or industrial capital had a hierarchical, pyramidal topology with clear centres and defined peripheries. This topology had an important property: it was attackable from the margins. Revolutions, reforms and social movements historically worked because the hierarchical structure had identifiable points of entry such as the king, the pope or the factory owner.

The scale-free network is structurally different. It has no centre that can be attacked because it has no centre in the hierarchical sense. It has hubs, yet the hubs are distributed and redundant, and if one is weakened, traffic redistributes across the others. It is an architecture originally designed by DARPA for military networks to resist attacks by eliminating single points of failure.

Technological power inherited this architecture and elevated it to a level of complexity the original military networks never imagined. Redundancy is not only geographical but functional, economic and legal. If one country regulates Google, traffic redistributes across infrastructures in other jurisdictions. If one service is constrained, the ecosystem adapts through alternatives generated by the network itself.

Critical Nodes

Yet the network is not uniformly resistant. The scale-free topology has a specific vulnerability. While it is robust to random attacks, it is fragile to targeted attacks on hubs. A perturbation aimed at the most connected nodes can fragment the entire network.

The critical nodes of technological power are the infrastructural layers on which companies rest, not the companies themselves. Computational infrastructure, chips, data centres and physical networks form the deepest and least redundant layer. TSMC produces the majority of the world's advanced chips on a single geographically vulnerable island. AWS, Azure and Google Cloud together control most of global cloud computing. These are the nodes on which all other hubs depend.

An effective perturbation of technological power would therefore target not applications such as social networks, search engines or e-commerce platforms, but the underlying infrastructure. That is where the network is least redundant, least adaptable and most vulnerable to regulatory or geopolitical intervention.

AI and Dynamic Topology

Pre-AI technological power had a relatively stable topology. The structure of the network changed slowly through acquisitions, mergers and market expansion. Artificial intelligence introduces a qualitative discontinuity by giving the network the capacity to reconfigure itself.

An AI system that optimises traffic distribution, allocates computational resources or decides which content reaches which users is modifying the topology of the network in real time. It is no longer a network with a fixed form that can be analysed statically. It is a network that redraws itself continuously in response to perturbations, anticipating and neutralising interventions before they produce structural effects.

This is the qualitative leap that makes contemporary technological power unlike any previous form of power. It is not only larger or more connected. It is adaptive in the sense that it can modify its own topology to preserve its underlying structure.

The consequence for anyone seeking to perturb it is precise: static analytical tools, antitrust laws, sectoral regulations and parliamentary inquiries act on a snapshot of a network that has already reconfigured itself. What is needed are dynamic tools capable of tracking topology in motion, and such tools do not yet exist.
